Mind Manual

This is a collection of teachings regarding meditation and the processes Mind appears to be functioning in.

This is intended as a "practice manual" taken up by the author and shared freely with others.

It is not a "book" in the traditional sense, but a collection of teachings and practices that can be used to deepen one's understanding of Mind written in HTML. Being in HTML, these resources are not limited.

This work is ongoing, as continuous iteration is the result of continued practice.

This work follows a format found often in Buddhism in which teachings are relayed in numbered symmetric pairs. As such, the first teach is on one topic "Buddha," the second on two topics which are themselves pairs of two, and so on for each consecutive topic.

  1. Buddha
  2. Entering Meditation
    1. Calming (shamatha) & Intuiting (vipassanā)
    2. Searching (vitarka) & Reflecting (vicara)
  3. Meditation wrt Suffering
    1. Craving (tṛṣṇā):
      • Gratification (bhoga)
      • Existence (bhava)
      • Non-existence (vibhava)
    2. Poisons (viṣa):
      • Greed (lobha)
      • Hatred (dveṣa)
      • Delusion (moha)
    3. Disorders (doṣa):
      • Wind (vāta)
      • Coldness (śīta)
      • Dampness (aṃbhās)
  4. Meditation that ends Suffering
    1. The Four Foundations of Mindfulness:
      1. Mindfulness of the body (kāyānupasthāna)
      2. Mindfulness of feelings or sensations (vedanānupasthāna)
      3. Mindfulness of mind or consciousness (cittānupasthāna)
      4. Mindfulness of mental objects or phenomena (dhammānupasthāna)
    2. The Four Immeasurables:
      1. Loving-kindness (maitrī)
      2. Compassion (karuṇā)
      3. Empathetic Joy (muditā)
      4. Equanimity (upekṣā)
    3. The Four Jhānas:
      1. The First Dhyāna (prathamadhyāna): Detached pleasure and joy (prīti-sukha).
      2. The Second Dhyāna (dvitīyadhyāna): Inner tranquility and unity of mind (ekaggatā).
      3. The Third Dhyāna (tṛtīyadhyāna): Contentment (santuṣṭi), mindful equanimity (upekṣā), and clear awareness.
      4. The Fourth Dhyāna (caturthadhyāna): Purity of equanimity (upekṣā) and mindfulness, neither pain nor pleasure.
    4. The Four Formless Realms:
      1. The realm of infinite space (ākāśānantyāyatana)
      2. The realm of infinite consciousness (vijñānānantyāyatana)
      3. The realm of nothingness (ākiñcanyāyatana)
      4. The realm of neither perception nor non-perception (naiva saṃjñānāsaṃjñāyatana)
  5. Meditation wrt Enlightenment
    1. The Five Precepts:
      1. Abstention from taking life (ahimsa).
      2. Abstention from taking what is not given (asteya).
      3. Abstention from sexual misconduct (brahmacharya).
      4. Abstention from false speech (satya).
      5. Abstention from intoxicants that cloud the mind (shaucha).
    2. The Five Aggregates:
      1. Form or matter (rūpa).
      2. Sensation or feeling (vedanā).
      3. Perception (samjñā).
      4. Mental formations (samskāra).
      5. Consciousness (vijñāna).
    3. The Five Powers:
      1. Confidence or faith (shraddhā).
      2. Energy or effort (vīrya).
      3. Mindfulness (smṛti).
      4. Concentration (samādhi).
      5. Wisdom or insight (prajñā).
    4. The Five Destinations:
      1. Hell beings (naraka).
      2. Hungry ghosts (preta).
      3. Animals (tiryagyoni).
      4. Humans (manuṣya).
      5. Gods or heavenly beings (deva).
    5. The Five Tathāgatas:
      1. Immovable One (Akṣobhya) in the east.
      2. Jewel Born One (Ratnasambhava) in the south.
      3. Infinite Light (Amitābha) in the west.
      4. Unobstructed Success (Amoghasiddhi) in the north.
      5. Great Sun or Illuminator (Vairocana) in the center.
  6. The Boddhisattva Discipline
    1. The Six Perfections (pāramitās):
      1. Generosity (dāna-pāramitā).
      2. Morality (śīla-pāramitā).
      3. Patience (kṣānti-pāramitā).
      4. Effort (vīrya-pāramitā).
      5. Concentration (dhyāna-pāramitā).
      6. Wisdom (prajñā-pāramitā).
    2. Six Underlying Tendencies (anusayas):
      1. Desire for Sensual Pleasure (kāma-rāga)
      2. Desire for Existence (bhava-rāga)
      3. Wrong Views (diṭṭhi)
      4. Doubt (vicikicchā)
      5. Conceit (māna)
      6. Ignorance (avijjā)
    3. The Six Realms (lokas):
      1. Gods or Divine Beings (deva-loka).
      2. Asuras or Demigods (asura-loka).
      3. Humans (manuṣya-loka).
      4. Animals (tiryak-loka).
      5. Hungry Ghosts (preta-loka).
      6. Hell Beings (naraka-loka).
    4. The Six Sense Bases (indriyas):
      1. Eyes (cakṣu-indriya).
      2. Ears (śrotra-indriya).
      3. Nose (ghrāṇa-indriya).
      4. Tongue (jihvā-indriya).
      5. Body (kāya-indriya).
      6. Mind (mana-indriya).
    5. The Six Perceptions (saṃjñās):
      1. Visual perception (rūpa-saṃjñā).
      2. Auditory perception (śabda-saṃjñā).
      3. Olfactory perception (gandha-saṃjñā).
      4. Gustatory perception (rasa-saṃjñā).
      5. Tactile perception (spraṣṭavya-saṃjñā).
      6. Mental perception (dharma-saṃjñā).
    6. The Six Elements (dhātus):
      1. Earth or Solidity (pṛthivī-dhātu).
      2. Water or Cohesion (ap-dhātu).
      3. Fire or Temperature (tejas-dhātu).
      4. Air or Motion (vāyu-dhātu).
      5. Space (ākāśa-dhātu).
      6. Consciousness (vijñāna-dhātu).
